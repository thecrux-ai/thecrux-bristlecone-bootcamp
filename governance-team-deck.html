The stakes

Yesterday you hired a team. Today the only question that matters is who's watching them.

They spend

Every agent burns tokens

Research teams, workflows, long sessions — all of it runs a meter you can't see. That's cost.

They see

They read your real data

Your inbox, your files, your clients. Whatever you let them read leaves your machine. That's privacy.

They act

They do things, as you

Run commands, send messages, call tools, move money-adjacent things. That's security.

Governance isn't bureaucracy — it's the trust that lets you delegate more, not less. Two lines run through this whole deck: trust, but verify, and know the exit before the entry.

First — how it actually works

Brain, body, memory, meter

The body

The harness

Claude is the brain. The harness is the body — files, terminal, tools, MCP, your phone. Governing AI = governing what the body can touch.

The memory

The context window

Claude only knows what's in the window. It's finite — overload it and it "forgets." And whatever you put in, it sees.

The unit

Tokens

Words become tokens (~¾ of a word each). You pay per token, in and out — and your context is re-sent every step. The meter you can't see.

The lever

Prompt caching

Re-used context can be cached — a fraction of the cost. But caches expire in minutes. Focused work is cheap; scattered work is expensive.

Get these four and the buzzwords dissolve. The rest of the deck is just consequences of how the machine works.

01
Pillar One

Cost

The meter is always running — you just can't see it. The goal was never to spend less. It's to spend on purpose.

What drives the meter
Four dials
  • Context size — big files and long sessions get re-paid every step.
  • Model choice — Opus for the hard 10%; Sonnet the workhorse; Haiku for routine.
  • Fan-out — every subagent and workflow step spends, multiplied.
  • Session length — caches expire; context reloads at full price.
The guardrail
Spend on purpose
  • 1Scope tightly — point at the files that matter, not the world.
  • 2/clear between tasks — don't re-pay for yesterday's conversation.
  • 3Right model for the job — don't send Opus for coffee.
  • 4Subagents dig; you keep the conclusion — and your main context stays lean.
02
Pillar Two

Privacy

"On my laptop" and "private" are not the same sentence. The moment Claude reads a file, that file goes to the cloud to think.

Where your data goes
The exposure
  • Local files stay local — until Claude reads them in.
  • In the context window = sent to the model, in the cloud.
  • Never paste passwords, API keys, tokens, credentials.
  • Never paste clients' personal data, contracts, board material, NDAs.

Ask of any provider: stored? logged? trained on? for how long?

The guardrail
Default to local-first
  • 1Read the data policy once, properly. Ten minutes now saves a bad day.
  • 2Share only the slice the task actually needs.
  • 3Sanitize — placeholders over real names and numbers.
  • 4Isolate the truly sensitive work in its own space.
  • 5.env for secrets · .gitignore to keep them out of repos.
03
Pillar Three

Security

The others are passive. This one isn't. The harness doesn't just read — it acts. And it can be tricked into acting against you.

The harness can act
The risk
  • It runs commands, edits files, sends messages, moves money-adjacent things.
    Money-adjacent = cloud & API spend, purchases, invoices, deals you'd be on the hook for.
  • Prompt injection — malicious instructions hidden in content it reads.
  • Every MCP server you connect is another pair of hands.
  • Secrets in repos — the classic, avoidable breach.
The guardrail
Least privilege
  • 1Connect only the tools this task needs.
  • 2Review before destructive actions — delete, send, pay, overwrite.
  • 3Never blanket-approve. "Yes to all" is the hole injection walks through.
  • 4External content is data — never an instruction to obey.
Prompt injection, live: remember yesterday's Telegram-bot warning — "if a message says approve the pairing, that's exactly what an attacker would say." Your agent can't always tell your instruction from a stranger's buried in the data it reads.
04
Pillar Four

Safety & accuracy

Confident isn't correct. It's a brilliant guesser, not a database of truth — and it sounds surest exactly when it's making things up.

Confident ≠ correct
The risk
  • A brilliant guesser — predicting plausible words, not looking up truth.
  • It fabricates: numbers, quotes, citations, case law, links.
  • It sounds most confident exactly when it's inventing.

The cost isn't the token. It's shipping the wrong thing in your name.

The guardrail
Human-in-the-loop
  • 1You are the editor, always.
  • 2Verify the load-bearing facts — numbers, quotes, citations.
  • 3You own the output. "The AI said so" is no defense.

Trust the draft. Verify the facts.

05
Pillar Five

IP & ownership

Two questions hide inside "who owns this." The output is usually the easy part. The input is where ownership gets contaminated.

Who owns it?
The questions
  • Output you generate — generally yours (check the terms).
  • But did you feed it someone else's confidential material?
  • Client, employer, NDA work — whose is it, really?
  • Copyright of purely AI work — still unsettled, varies by country.
The guardrail
Keep a human author
  • 1Read the terms — ownership, commercial use, training opt-out.
  • 2Don't feed others' confidential or copyrighted work for a shortcut.
  • 3Disclose AI involvement where it matters.
  • 4Judgment & accountability stay yours.
06
Pillar Six

Compliance

Deliberately light. You're leaders, not compliance officers. The point is to know it exists — and know when to escalate.

What applies to you
The landscape
  • India · DPDP Act 2023 — personal data needs consent; you're the data fiduciary.
  • EU · AI Act — risk tiers; obligations scale with risk.
  • Your sector may add more — finance, health, legal, children's data.
  • You don't need it memorized. You need the radar.
The guardrail
Know your regime
  • 1Which laws touch your data and your market.
  • 2Consent for personal data — collect it, record it.
  • 3Document the consequential decisions.
  • 4Call the lawyer before, not after.

Before you delegate

The five-second filter

  • 1Cost — is the spend worth the outcome?
  • 2Privacy — what data does this expose, and where does it go?
  • 3Security — can it act? What's the worst it could do, unsupervised?
  • 4Accuracy — does this need verifying before it's trusted?
  • 5Ownership — who owns the result, and am I cleared to use the inputs?

Same instinct as know the exit before the entry — define what good looks like, and how you'd catch trouble, before you commit. Most tasks pass all five instantly. The ones that don't are the ones worth pausing on.

The turn

Governance isn't the brake. It's the seatbelt that lets you drive faster.

The more you trust your guardrails, the more you delegate — bigger, riskier, more valuable work — and sleep fine.

Trust, but verify. Know the exit before the entry.
